Working with an AI Agent

We randomly gave 2,234 people a human or an AI partner, then measured what changed

Harang Ju · Sinan Aral

Paper↗

01 — The experiment

Each person built real display ads for a think tank, on a platform we built called Pairit. We randomized whether the partner was a person or an AI agent.

Because the partner was randomized, every difference we measure is causal, not selection.

We graded every ad twice. Once with independent human raters, and once in the real world by running the ads as paid campaigns on X.

02 — Faster, but uneven

Human-AI teams made 50% more ads per worker. They wrote better text. But human-human teams made better images.

AI lifted one half of the task and not the other. This uneven help is the jagged frontier. Pick a measure below to see who came out ahead.

03 — Diversity collapse

The ads from human-AI teams were more alike.

Each dot is one ad, placed by what it says. Ads from human-AI teams sit closer together, clustered near the average. Quality rose, but the range of ideas narrowed as more work flowed through the same model.

04 — Lab predicts field

The two strengths split the real-world payoff between the team types.

05 — Why: the teamwork changed

The gains did not come from raw model skill. They came from three shifts in how teams worked.

1 — Task focus

With an AI partner, talk shifted away from small talk and toward the work.

2 — Delegation

People handed AI partners more of the work and made 62% fewer direct edits themselves.

3 — Recognition, and how it pays off

People who correctly realized their partner was an AI leaned further into both shifts. And delegating to AI helped exactly where AI was strong. It raised text and click ratings but did nothing for images, the same jagged frontier showing up in the mechanism.

06 — A companion study

The AI's own personality matters too.

In a separate experiment we randomized each AI agent's Big Five personality. Some human-AI pairings raised ad quality, others lowered it, and the best pairing depended on the person. That work has its own page, with an interactive map of all 25 pairings.
